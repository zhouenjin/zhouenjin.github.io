Why PPO Replaced TRPO in Practice

A rewrite of my reinforcement learning notes on trust-region methods, focusing on the moment where a beautiful idea became an actually usable training workflow.

2026 Reinforcement Learning Derived from rl-notes

If I compress the transition from TRPO to PPO into one sentence, it is this: reinforcement learning did not stop needing safe policy updates, but it desperately needed a way to get them without paying the price of second-order optimization every single iteration.

That is why PPO matters. Not because it changed the core goal, but because it made that goal trainable with ordinary deep learning infrastructure. In practice, that difference is large enough to decide what people actually use.

The problem TRPO was trying to solve

Vanilla policy gradient methods have an awkward failure mode: the update direction may be correct, but the step can still be too large. Once the policy distribution moves too far in one iteration, performance can collapse. Small steps are safer, but then optimization becomes painfully slow.

TRPO addresses this with a very clean principle: do not let the new policy move too far away from the old one. Formally, it constrains the average KL divergence between old and new policy distributions and optimizes within that trust region.

TRPO is not only about where to move. It is equally about how far one can move safely.

This is an important conceptual step. It turned policy updates from a learning-rate tuning game into a constrained optimization problem. That idea survives almost unchanged in PPO.

Why TRPO is elegant but hard to use

The issue is not the idea. The issue is the machinery. Once the KL constraint is introduced, the actor update is no longer a simple first-order gradient step. A practical TRPO implementation typically needs Fisher-vector products, conjugate-gradient solves, line search, and careful numerical handling of all of them.

This makes TRPO intellectually satisfying but operationally expensive. It is harder to implement, harder to debug, and awkward to scale with the same ease as the rest of deep learning.

What PPO keeps and what it changes

PPO keeps the same high-level goal: improve the policy while controlling how much it changes. What it changes is the route. Instead of solving a constrained second-order problem directly, PPO rewrites the safety idea into a simple loss function.

The most influential version, PPO-Clip, uses the importance ratio between the new and old policy, r_t = pi_new(a|s) / pi_old(a|s), and then clips it into a narrow interval such as [1 - epsilon, 1 + epsilon]. The optimization always takes the conservative branch of the two possible objectives.

Intuitively, the rule is easy to remember:

  • If an action is good, increase its probability, but not too much
  • If an action is bad, decrease its probability, but not too much
  • Never trust a large ratio jump, even when it looks beneficial

The real engineering win

The deepest practical reason PPO replaced TRPO is that it converts trust-region thinking into a loss that standard optimizers can handle. Once that happens, the rest of the workflow becomes much more familiar:

  • collect on-policy trajectories
  • compute returns and GAE advantages
  • optimize actor and critic with ordinary mini-batch updates
  • repeat for multiple epochs on the same batch

That last point matters a lot. PPO can safely reuse the same on-policy batch for multiple epochs, while TRPO usually does one expensive actor update and then needs fresh data again. This is one of the main reasons PPO feels dramatically more usable.

Why “multiple epochs” does not make PPO off-policy

This is one of the easiest mistakes to make when first learning PPO. The fact that PPO trains for multiple epochs on the same data does not turn it into an off-policy algorithm.

The key distinction is not whether data is reused inside the current iteration. The key distinction is whether data from older policies is carried across iterations. PPO does not do that. After the current optimization round, the old batch is discarded and the next round starts from newly collected trajectories.

How I now remember the TRPO to PPO transition

My current mental model is simple:

  • REINFORCE says: optimize the policy directly
  • Actor-Critic says: use a critic to reduce variance
  • TRPO says: constrain the update so the policy does not jump too far
  • PPO says: keep the same safety instinct, but package it into a trainable loss

Seen this way, PPO is less a theoretical replacement than an implementation victory. It is what happens when a principled idea is rewritten to fit the tools that practitioners already know how to scale.

What still matters when implementing PPO

Even though PPO is much simpler than TRPO, its success still depends on a few engineering habits that are easy to overlook:

  • normalize advantages before actor updates
  • keep the policy batch on-policy and discard it after training
  • monitor KL drift even when using clipping
  • treat the clip coefficient as a stability-speed tradeoff
  • separate the conceptual role of gamma and lambda

Closing thought

Reading TRPO and PPO side by side changed how I think about reinforcement learning progress. Sometimes progress is not a new objective or a new theorem. Sometimes it is the translation of a good idea into a form the entire ecosystem can actually use.

That is why PPO became the default choice across game environments, robotics pipelines, and large-model RLHF workflows. It did not abandon the trust-region intuition. It made that intuition deployable.
